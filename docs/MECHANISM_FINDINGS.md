# Density Classification — Mechanism Findings

This note records diagnostic results from probing what Moore-neighborhood
NCCAs actually *do* to the lattice, and what that implies for the 2D Fukś
construction. Results were generated by the scripts

- [mechanism_probe_58ed6.py](mechanism_probe_58ed6.py)
- [coarsening_scan.py](coarsening_scan.py)
- [finite_switch_coarsener_test.py](finite_switch_coarsener_test.py)

## 1. The current best rule does not phase-separate or canonicalize

`sid:58ed6b657afb` alone, run without any amplifier on random near-critical
tori, reaches a frozen attractor in ~64 steps. At the attractor:

| L | decodability (Moore-agree ∩ VN-agree) | interface / L² | largest majority-component | components |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 64  | 0.19 | 1.01 | 0.14 | 27 |
| 128 | 0.17 | 1.01 | 0.20 | 42 |
| 256 | 0.15 | 1.01 | 0.27 | 56 |

- Density is conserved (NCCA sanity check).
- Local-majority agreement with the *true global majority* **decreases** from
  ~0.42 at random init to ~0.17 at the attractor.
- Interface length per site stays near 1.0 — **no coarsening**.
- The attractor is a fragmented, frozen state that *actively destroys*
  majority decodability.

The same pattern holds for the other leading candidates
(`sid:812b7dae7aa7`, `sid:029b09cea0b5`, `sid:29154e9615d8`,
`sid:ac809b9cab03`): none coarsens. The strongest equalizers are in fact
**fragmenters** that drive interface up to 1.4–1.8.

## 2. The success of repeated blocks is emergent, not preprocessing

`[sid:58ed6 ⁸, VN-maj¹⁶]^k` reaches 96–100% consensus on 128–256² tori.
Since sid:58ed6 alone does not produce a majority-friendly state, the
success is not "F canonicalizes, then M decodes". It is the emergent
dynamics of F and M *alternating* that matters. Viewed stroboscopically,
this is a single larger-radius CA — consistent with the methodological note
that periodic schedules reduce to a single CA.

Practical consequence: the empirical strength of the repeated-block track
is **not evidence for a Fukś-style extension**. It is evidence for a
heuristic CA baseline of the same kind ruled out by the standard
impossibility framework.

## 3. Coarseners exist in the wider catalog — but are not black/white balanced

A random 5000-rule scan of the 133,713-rule catalog at L=64, T=128 found
genuine coarseners:

| sid | final interface / L² | largest majority component | decodability-min-side |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| `11bb6b4211627713` | 0.20 | 0.14 | 0.51 |
| `a2f18d758cde8278` | 0.26 | 0.11 | 0.51 |
| `32f259e26453690a` | 0.36 | 0.50 | 0.52 |
| `f7d3b4a4f4701460` | 0.31 | 0.23 | 0.51 |

These rules do reduce interface by 4–5× and produce large clusters.
**However**, their decodability-min-side stays ≈ 0.51 (barely above random)
and — crucially — they are *asymmetric between `p = 0.49` and `p = 0.51`*.
Rule `sid:32f259e26453690a` grows a huge cluster only at p=0.49, not at
p=0.51. These rules coarsen, but to a state whose dominant cluster color
is **not reliably the global-majority color**.

## 4. Moore / VN majority as amplifier has metastable fixed points

For all coarseners tested, the full schedule `F^L → M^T2` gives
`correct_consensus_rate = 0.0` even at **T2 = 1024** (8× the usual length),
on 64² through 128². The final-majority accuracy (correct density-side) is
high (0.93–1.00) but the lattice does not reach consensus. These are true
fixed points of Moore/VN majority, not slow convergence — longer time does
not help.

Structural reason: 2D Moore/VN majority has a huge family of stable fixed
points — any long straight stripe of uniform color is invariant, as are
rectangular blocks and many "maze" patterns. The basin of attraction of
all-0 or all-1 is *narrow* in initial-configuration space.

This is the real bottleneck. No preprocessor alone can solve the task if
its output is not in the narrow basin of all-consensus under plain local
majority.

## 5. Implications for the Fukś-analog problem

Given the user's formulation
`(F_1^{t_1} ∘ … ∘ F_k^{t_k})^T  then  majority^T`:

1. **Single-catalog-rule finite-switch schedules do not reach perfect
   classification** in any tested candidate. This includes both coarseners
   and the repeated-block champions. The limiting factor is the narrow
   basin of plain local majority, not the preprocessor.

2. **Periodic composition inside the preprocessing phase is equivalent to
   a single CA with larger effective radius.** Compositions of 2–3 NCCAs
   therefore search a space of larger-radius NCCAs that is not in the
   133k-rule catalog. This is the richest unexplored direction that
   matches the user's formulation.

3. **The repeated-block track `[F M]^k` is methodologically
   indistinguishable from finite-switch:** both are CAs at larger effective
   radius. The repeated-block track works empirically; the single-F
   finite-switch track does not. This suggests that inserting majority
   steps inside the preprocessing phase adds a *specific* dynamical
   mechanism (interfacing curvature flow with NCCA transport) that is hard
   to replicate with pure NCCA composition. But that mechanism exits the
   user's formulation.

4. **Even theoretically, perfect decodability (`moore_majority_agreement = 1`)
   is density-limited.** At minority density > 4/9 ≈ 0.44, *no*
   configuration has every 3×3 window containing ≥ 5 majority cells. So
   for densities in (0.44, 0.5), at least one majority step is not enough,
   and the state must be further iterated.

## 6. Pair-composition search — also negative

Ran ordered pair compositions
`(F_1^{t_1} ∘ F_2^{t_2})^T → VN-majority^{T_2}` over 11 top candidate sids
(coarseners + fragmenters + outer-monotone best) at 32² with 5 budgets
`(t_1, t_2, T)` — 550 schedules total.

**Result: `min_correct_consensus_rate = 0.000` for every pair and every
budget.** All schedules reach `min_final_majority_accuracy = 1.0` (correct
decoded answer on both sides) but none reach consensus.

Ran ordered pair multi-phase schedules
`F_1^{32} ∘ F_2^{32} → VN-majority^{1024}` over 90 ordered pairs at grids
32 and 64 (180 runs). **Same result: zero correct consensus rate
everywhere.** For some pairs, `min_final_majority_accuracy` drops below
0.5, meaning the preprocessor composition flips majority to the wrong
side — an active failure mode, not just a stuck state.

See [composition_search_pilot.json](composition_search_pilot.json) and
[multiphase_pair_long.json](multiphase_pair_long.json) for full results.

## 7. Structural conclusion

Within the user's formulation — `(F_1^{t_1} ∘ … ∘ F_k^{t_k})^T → majority^{T_m}`
with plain local (Moore or VN) majority as the amplifier — **no tested
schedule achieves nonzero correct consensus at even 32² scale**. This is
despite:

- 5000 random single rules × finite-switch with T₁ ∝ L and T₂ up to 1024;
- 57 outer-monotone rules × the same;
- 55 ordered pairs of top candidates × 5 budgets;
- 90 multi-phase ordered pairs × long-tail T₂ = 1024.

The bottleneck is the **metastable fixed-point set of 2D majority**. Any
configuration containing a long straight interface of opposite colors is a
fixed point of Moore and VN majority. After an NCCA preprocessor of any
known type, the lattice contains such interfaces, and majority freezes
there. Longer T₂ does not help — these are true fixed points, not slow
convergence.

Contrast with 1D: majority-of-3 on a ring has **no non-consensus
fixed points** besides the period-2 checkerboard. Rule 184 drives any
non-exactly-half-density configuration to a fixed point with no
"10" patterns, which is a single application away from consensus under
majority-of-3. The entire 1D Fukś mechanism rests on majority-of-3's
trivial fixed-point set, which 2D majority lacks.

## 8. Implications and recommendations

**(a) Perfect 2D classification within the strict formulation is likely
impossible using plain Moore/VN majority as the amplifier.** The evidence
is now strong across ~6000 preprocessor trials + ~700 composition trials.
Before declaring this formally impossible, the remaining untested
directions are:

1. Full-catalog (133k-rule) scan with the correct-consensus objective —
   brute-force confirmation. Budget: hours of numpy batched simulation.
2. Triples `(F_1, F_2, F_3)` — but by the same methodological note,
   k-rule periodic compositions are still single larger-radius CAs, so
   this explores a bigger but structurally similar space.
3. Very large T₁ (e.g., T₁ ~ L²) — to see if asymptotic NCCA fixed points
   differ from what the T₁ ~ L probe reached.

**(b) Relaxing the amplifier is the natural next move.** Two options
worth exploring even if formally outside "plain local majority":

1. **Larger-radius majority** (5×5 Moore, sum ≥ 13 of 25). Fewer fixed
   points than 3×3 Moore. Local, translation-invariant, still "a kind of
   majority." This is the closest relaxation to the user's formulation.
2. **Two-round majority** (Moore then VN or VN then Moore iterated).
   The composition has different fixed points than either alone.

**(c) The repeated-block track remains the strongest empirical result and
is consistent with the user's formulation *if* periodic repetition of the
entire `(NCCA, M)` pattern counts.** Per the methodological note in
[DENSITY_CLASSIFICATION_IDEAS.md](DENSITY_CLASSIFICATION_IDEAS.md), it
reduces to a single CA with larger effective radius, so it cannot solve
2D DCP perfectly — but it reaches 96–100% at 256², which may be "good
enough" for many purposes. It should not be dropped.

**(d) The formulation itself needs a decision from the user.**
Given the structural finding, one of these must be true:

- Option 1: There exists some catalog NCCA F and schedule we have not yet
  tested that does the job. This requires full-catalog-scale search and is
  a long shot given the evidence.
- Option 2: No single-NCCA finite-switch schedule works; we must allow
  compositions, and larger-k compositions exist that solve it. Also a
  long shot given the pair results.
- Option 3: 2D plain-majority is too restrictive as an amplifier, and the
  user's formulation should be generalized to include a broader class of
  local amplifiers.
- Option 4: The answer is probabilistic or asynchronous (Toom-like), not
  deterministic CA. Outside the current scope.
